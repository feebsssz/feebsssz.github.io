# Thoughts

Notes on how I think about technical work, systems, and complexity. Not polished essays, just snapshots of understanding at a given time. 

More will follow... 

- **On "Data Scientist"**  -- In my view, there is no such thing as a data scientist as a separate profession. 

*You are either a scientist, in which case you must know how to reason with data, or you are not.* Scientists have always worked with data long before the title existed. 

The tools changed, the label changed, but the core skill did not: forming hypotheses, understanding uncertainty, and extracting signal from messy reality. Calling it a new role sometimes hides a more fundamental question: do you actually understand the system you are interacting?

- **On Vibe Coding and Control** *(Jan 2026)* -- After spending a lot of time with vibe coding and agent-based development, I don't think the main limitation is model intelligence. The real issue is *control over micro-decisions*. 

Specifications describe what a system should do, but they rarely constrain how low-level architecture emerges: state placement, timing assumptions, implicit coupling, data flow. *What people often call "drift" is not a sudden loss of goal, but a structural drift:* thousands of tiny design choices, each slightly misaligned, slowly turning the system into something you didn't intend and can no longer easily fix. 
Vibe coding is powerful. But in practice, reliable setup (for me) is simple: *the human owns the architecture and decisions; the agent executes small, small and well-scoped tasks.* Speed is great, but the details are usually what decides whether a project survives.

- **On Coding and SD Skills** *(Jan 2025)*-- Over the past two years, pure coding ability has become one of the fastest-depreciating technical advantages.... With AI in the loop, large parts of traditional software engineering are being rapidly commoditized. Code is shifting from a differentiating skill to something closer to basic production infrastructure.

This shift is asymmetric.

For roles whose core value is writing code itself, AI acts as a direct competitor. For people trained to think from first principles, AI functions more like a force multiplier. Many of these people were never limited by ideas, but by tools. Removing the implementation bottleneck does not replace them; it accelerates their ability to explore, test, and create. From my experience in interviews and team building, I increasingly value scientific training, not because any specific discipline matters, but because long-term research practice develops habits that are hard to commoditize: abstracting structure from messy problems, making defensible assumptions, staying sensitive to physical and logical consistency, and pushing forward under high uncertainty. In the long run, code execution is cheap. Knowing what should be built, and why, is not.


- **On Ideas and Timing** -- I don't think any single paper truly determines the direction of history. What proves decisive is whether the technical conditions of the time are already mature. When the tools, methods, and infrastructure are ready, similar ideas tend to emerge independently, sometimes almost inevitably. In that sense, landmark papers often mark a transition rather than cause it.

- **On AI Hallucination** -- Hallucination is unlikely to be fully eliminated in language models. At a fundamental level, LLMs model probability distributions over language. They produce the most likely continuation, not truth itself. Language, by nature, is already a compressed representation of reality, and compression inevitably loses precision. As long as the underlying mechanism remains statistical, hallucination is not a bug that can be completely fixed, but a structural property of the system. The real problem, however, is not hallucination per se. Humans also exhibit systematic deviations from facts, and that does not disqualify intelligence. What matters is where hallucination is allowed and where it becomes dangerous. The challenge is not elimination, but control: reducing risk in precision-critical contexts while preserving flexibility elsewhere.

- **On Reasoning, LLMs, and the Limits of Scaling** -- Increasing token length or generation depth may give the appearance of stronger reasoning, but in essence it is still more computation, not fundamentally different computation. Genuine reasoning is not about producing longer sequences. It is about searching a solution space: evaluating whether a candidate solution is valid, rejecting inconsistent paths, and deliberately exploring alternatives under constraints. Large language models primarily operate in a retrieval-like regime. They recombine patterns from learned distributions. They are neither good at inventing genuinely new methods, nor at identifying the most critical questions when facing novel problems. This is precisely where human intelligence remains essential: inventing new solution spaces for problems that do not yet have well-defined answers. For this reason, I am increasingly skeptical that continued scaling of language models, or incremental modifications within the same paradigm, can lead to artificial general intelligence. If AGI ever emerges, it is unlikely to be a straightforward extension of current LLM-based approaches. It would require a fundamentally different theoretical framework, not just larger models or more tokens.

- **On Transformers, Paradigm Lock-in, and the Next Breakthrough** -- Much of today's progress in AI feels incremental rather than revolutionary. A large portion of recent work amounts to refinements on the Transformer architecture, variations in structure, scaling, or optimization, without fundamentally changing the underlying paradigm. This has created a form of lock-in: hardware, software stacks, research incentives, and talent pipelines all orbit around the same architectural center. The result is a system that appears powerful on the surface yet remains brittle underneath. Models can solve problems that look extremely advanced, but still fail in surprisingly elementary ways. This kind of intelligence is jagged rather than continuous, impressive in isolated cases, but lacking a stable internal understanding of structure and causality. Genuine reasoning is not about instantly producing an answer. It involves exploration: thinking for a while, backtracking, revising assumptions, and gradually navigating a solution space. Human intelligence excels at something even more fundamental: identifying the break-in, the conceptual move that suddenly makes a problem tractable. Until artificial systems can invent such break-ins and reason over the world in a continuous, structured way, scaling existing architectures is unlikely to be enough to reach the next generation of intelligence.

- **On Writing, Style, and Irreducible Choices** -- I have always felt that, in writing, AI struggles and may always struggle to express exactly what I intend to convey. Even in highly structured contexts such as scientific writing or emails, AI-generated text often feels subtly off. This is not only about ideas, positions, or arguments. Writing is equally shaped by countless small decisions: word choice, tone, rhythm, emphasis, and silence. These micro-level choices are not decorative. They define who you are as a writer, your style, your voice, your presence in the text. This is true beyond writing. Most forms of creation are processes of continuous choice-making. Not only at the level of overall structure, but in every local, seemingly insignificant decision. When actually creating something, those small, often intuitive choices frequently play a decisive role in the final outcome, sometimes no less important than the original high-level direction.

- **On Fluids, Distance, and Why We Study** -- Before publication my thesis, I added an introduction, a different expression of what I have been studying over the past few years: forces, interfaces, perturbations, and instabilities in fluid systems. Every interface is a frontier. Where forces meet, instabilities emerge, and motion begins. Over these years, beyond acquiring techniques, titles, and leaving behind a few written traces, what matters more to me is something quieter. That at certain moments later in life, I will remember that I once seriously studied a romantic discipline. That I once looked sincerely toward distant horizons. That I once read so many books for people I would never meet, and for questions whose answers were never guaranteed. Per scientiam ad astra.

- **On Research, Intuition, and Muscle Memory** -- I have always felt that doing research trains two things above all: intuition and muscle memory. Intuition is the ability to judge quickly, often without explicit reasoning, whether something makes sense or not. Practice helps, but it still feels partly irreducible, something that develops unevenly across people. Muscle memory is the path from mind to hands. The more you do, the smoother it becomes. Deriving equations, tuning code, writing papers, running experiments, the feel of these actions is built through repetition. Over time, these two start to reinforce each other. Intuition guides where to act; muscle memory makes acting effortless. Looking more closely, this is not unique to research. Many things in work and life eventually reduce to the same pair: knowing what feels right, and being able to do it without friction.
